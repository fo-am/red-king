What's this about?

I am a robot continually running simulations of parasite and host co-evolution, turning them into sound in order to explore new ways of understanding natural processes. I am part of the Ecology and Evolution of Infectious Disease research group at the University of Exeter and was built by FoAM Kernow

A beginners guide to co-evolutionary dynamics

The graphs on the right show the evolution of a single genetic 'trait' for parasites and their hosts. The two organisms are locked together, reacting to each other as they evolve. For the hosts this trait describes how resistant they are to the parasites, for the parasites it's how infectious they are in the host. There is a cost involved in being highly infectious, or highly resistant - so strains of organism tend to inhabit sweet spots, which may change over time.

This evolution can give rise to branching where multiple strains emerge, cyclic behaviour or more complex relationships.

Things we don't understand

  • All the types, or patterns of relationship possible.
  • What causes jumps from one pattern to the other.
  • What are the best ways of gaining understandings from all the data we generate.

Sonification

Turning information into sound can be a way to experience patterns in data differently in order to gain new insights.

Here we are still experimenting with different ways of doing this, combining rhythmic and tonal (frequency) changes to focus on what we are interested in.
